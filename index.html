The Doris Duke Foundation and the Performing Arts Technologies Lab

The Performing Arts Technologies Lab is an initiative by the Doris Duke Foundation, awarding grants to 20 innovative projects that explore how technology—especially AI—can enhance performing arts like theater, jazz, contemporary dance, and multi-disciplinary performances.

Featured Project: Totentanz (T3)

About Totentanz (T3)

Totentanz, also known as T3, is an innovative use of pose estimation and facial recognition technologies designed to prevent AI theft and unauthorized training that occurs without choreographer and dancer consent.

Our Team and Sponsorship

This solution was devised by Pedram Nimreezi, an AI engineer, and Nicole Perry, a dance and theatre artist. Momentum Stage is sponsoring the project, and it is funded through the inaugural Performing Arts Technologies Lab grant from the Doris Duke Charitable Foundation.

Doris Duke Foundation Announces Grants to 20 Innovative Projects

September 25, 2024

The Doris Duke Foundation announced grants through its inaugural Performing Arts Technologies Lab, supporting 20 pioneering artists, technologists, and arts organizations. These grants aim to develop new uses for digital technologies across the performing arts.

"Today is an important milestone for the Doris Duke Foundation as we proudly recognize these remarkable artists and organizations who have looked beyond their own challenges in search of solutions to meet the needs of an entire industry," said Sam Gill, president and CEO of the Doris Duke Foundation.

The selected projects tackle a wide range of opportunities in how the performing arts are produced, distributed, and consumed, including:

  • Exploring the use of emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence in creating, presenting, experiencing, and preserving works across the performing arts, particularly among artists representing underserved populations and cultures.
  • Enabling artists and audience members with disabilities to further access, create, and engage with art through the development of new digital tools.
  • Preserving inherently ephemeral works in the performing arts through new archiving technologies, fulfilling a need that is especially acute for Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC) artists.
  • Pursuing field-wide solutions to pressing issues such as capital needs.

"The more than 700 applications we received for this program sent us a clear message: performing artists are ready and excited to push the boundaries of what technology can do. They are asking critical questions about open-source approaches, accessibility, and representation. Our goal is to help these innovators do what artists do best—bringing new, powerful experiences to audiences." — Ashley Ferro-Murray, Doris Duke Foundation Arts Program Director.

Grant Recipients in the Inaugural Performing Arts Technologies Lab

The following are some of the grant recipients:

  • Alaska Native Heritage Center, Anchorage, AK – Dance
    Yuraq Project
    The Yuraq Project seeks to use motion capture technology to develop 3D asset files that will be incorporated into XR programs on-site at the Alaska Native Heritage Center.
  • Momentum Stage, Inc. with Nicole Perry, Pedram Nimreezi, and Ed Talavera, Plantation, FL – Dance
    Totentanz: Creative Performance Protection (aka T3)
    Totentanz (aka T3) is a new use of pose estimation and facial recognition technologies to decrease AI theft and training that occurs without choreographer and dancer consent.
