Increasing the Creative Agency of Artists through Advanced Technology

About Totentanz (T3)

What is Totentanz?

Totentanz (T3) leverages pose estimation and facial recognition technologies to prevent AI theft and unauthorized training of AI models without the consent of choreographers and dancers. Developed by AI engineer Pedram Nimreezi and dance artist Nicole Perry, T3 empowers artists by safeguarding their creative works.

Our Mission

The goal of Totentanz is to enhance the ownership and creative agency of artists. By providing tools and a platform for choreographers to store and share their videos securely, T3 ensures that their intellectual property is protected while still promoting access to dance performances globally through dancers.ai (coming March 2025).

Our Team and Sponsorship

Totentanz was developed by Pedram Nimreezi, an AI engineer, and Nicole Perry, a dance and theatre artist. Filmmaker Ed Talavera supports the initial research and development by filming dances to create the T3 data set. Momentum Stage sponsors the project, and it is funded through the inaugural “Performing Arts and Technologies Lab” grant from the Doris Duke Charitable Foundation.

Performing Arts Technologies Lab Grant

The Performing Arts Technologies Lab, funded by the Doris Duke Charitable Foundation, is a grant and support system designed to expand access and nurture new methods for creating, sharing, and experiencing performing arts. Momentum Stage sponsors the T3 project under this initiative.

Applications for the 2024 Lab are now closed. The Lab supports innovative ideas in jazz, contemporary dance, and theater that utilize new digital tools and production methods.

For full details, visit the Doris Duke Foundation's announcement.

Technological Impact on Performing Arts

AI and emerging technologies are revolutionizing the performing arts by assisting in choreography, analyzing motion patterns, and providing real-time feedback. Totentanz (T3) exemplifies this transformation by protecting artists' creative works while enhancing performance analytics and audience engagement.

Through cross-disciplinary collaborations, projects like T3 are creating rich, multi-disciplinary works that integrate dancers, musicians, and technologists to push the boundaries of artistic expression.

The history of the performing arts is intertwined with technological advancements. From theater design, lighting, rigging, and sound to the integration of digital processes on stage, these innovations continually provide artists with new forms of expression and audiences with new forms of experience.

COVID-19 accelerated the role of digital technology in the arts, leading to creative reconceptions of performances and audience engagement. The Doris Duke Foundation aims to support sustainable and effective use of digital technology in performing arts where it is artistically beneficial and creatively productive.
